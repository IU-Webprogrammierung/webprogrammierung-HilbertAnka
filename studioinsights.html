Studio Insights

Scenic artists typically work in a standing position while the piece lies flat on the floor. Large-scale application is carried out using long-handled brushes, brooms, and tools designed for broad, gestural work. Working at a distance is essential, as it maintains an overall view and aligns the process with the audience’s sight line in the auditorium. The focus lies on optical impact rather than fine detail: compositions are designed to be read from a distance, prioritising strong shapes, clear forms, and reduced micro-detail.

  • Preparation

    The fabric is stretched out on the floor at precise right angles, pulled tight and as smooth as possible. A primer is then applied using a spray gun or brush. This process seals the fabric, limiting absorbency and closing its surface pores.

  • Sketch Transfer

    A grid system is used to transfer the sketch onto the backdrop. Both the reference image and the canvas are divided into an equal number of squares. The motif is then drawn square by square in charcoal, allowing for easy corrections if needed.

  • Inking

    The charcoal sketch is fixed to prevent it from being lost during the painting process. The individual lines are traced over with paint and, if required, initial shadow areas are also established. In scenic painting, this process is called inking.

  • Painting Stage

    Following inking, colour is applied using either transparent or opaque techniques depending on the motif. A semi-opaque base layer is often used first to establish a stable foundational colour.
    Subsequent glazing layers are then built up to soften transitions, reduce hard edges, and subtly shift the overall colour direction.

  • Painting Stage

    In theatre painting, highly opaque applications are generally avoided to maintain the flexibility of the fabric. Since the material is subject to significant stress during storage and transport, thick paint layers can lead to cracking.
    Spray guns are frequently used as well, as they allow for an even, finely atomised application of paint, making them more efficient and consistent than brush or broom techniques.

  • Highlights

    The final stage involves adding highlights that bring the backdrop to life. Light tones, often extending to white, are used to enhance volume and spatial depth.
    These details create a sense of physical presence; without them, the image can appear flat. However, highlights should be applied with restraint, as a few targeted accents are more effective than excessive detailing.
